Why Buyers Choose Forest City: The Island Lifestyle Nobody Talks About

Sam Tee Property Advisor · REN 80322
Forest City, Iskandar Puteri, Johor

Almost every article written about Forest City focuses on investment risk, SFZ incentives, or the ghost town question. Almost none focuses on what it actually feels like to be there — the mangrove air, the sound of the sea, the golf course designed by Jack Nicklaus stretching to the horizon. This article is about that.

An island, not just a development

Forest City is built on four man-made islands in the Strait of Johor. That geographical fact changes everything about the living experience in ways that are difficult to appreciate until you have stood at the water's edge and looked across toward the Indonesian coast on one side and the Johor skyline on the other.

Most property developments in Johor — however well-designed — exist within a larger urban fabric. Noise, traffic, commercial sprawl, the visual clutter of a city growing fast. Forest City does not have any of that as your immediate neighbour. Your immediate neighbour is the ocean, the golf course, or the mangrove corridor. That is a fundamentally different living environment from any mainland township, regardless of how mature or well-managed it is.

The mangrove corridor: 2.86 million square metres of living ecosystem

Adjoining the island is one of the largest remaining mangrove green corridors in the southern Johor region — 2.86 million square metres of ecosystem that has been preserved and integrated into the Forest City environment rather than cleared for development. Guided mangrove ecotours operate within the corridor via registered boat operators, and the ecosystem hosts over 400 documented plant and animal species including migratory birds, monitor lizards, otters, and the full diversity of mangrove-associated marine life.

For residents, this means the greenery immediately surrounding the island is not manicured resort landscaping — it is a functioning tropical ecosystem. The air quality and biodiversity that comes with living adjacent to a healthy mangrove system is something that cannot be replicated in a built-up township environment.

Two world-ranked golf courses on your doorstep

The Forest City Golf Resort is the centrepiece of the island's lifestyle infrastructure — and its credentials are independently verified, not developer marketing.

The Classic Course has been ranked in the Top 100 Golf Courses in Asia for seven consecutive years from 2020 to 2026. Seven consecutive years is not a one-off recognition — it is a sustained acknowledgment of course quality that places Forest City alongside the most respected golf destinations in the region. The resort hosts over 100 international golf tournaments annually, which means the course is under competitive scrutiny from serious players throughout the year.

The Legacy Course, designed by Jack Nicklaus and his son Jack Nicklaus II, ranked 49th in the Asia-Pacific Top 100 Golf Courses in the 2024–2025 rankings published by China's largest golf platform, Cloud Golf. A Jack Nicklaus-designed course ranked in Asia's top 50 is a significant course design credential — courses of this calibre typically anchor resort communities that cost multiples of what Forest City currently prices at.

Residents in the Golf Villa cluster receive a five-year complimentary membership covering both courses. For buyers who play golf seriously, the lifestyle value of two ranked courses within walking distance of your front door is one of Forest City's clearest differentiators from any other Johor development.

Five-star hotel infrastructure open to residents

Forest City has two five-star properties on the island — the Forest City Marina Hotel and the Forest City Golf Hotel. For residents, this means the pool, gym, spa, and dining facilities of full-service luxury hotels are accessible as part of daily life rather than a destination visit. The resort pool shown in the photographs above is the Golf Hotel's main pool — open water views, tropical landscaping, genuine resort quality.

This is an unusual characteristic of the Forest City environment: the lifestyle infrastructure was built to hotel standard because the development was designed as a resort community from the outset. Most township developments build amenities after the residential product is sold. Forest City built the amenities first — and they remain in operation regardless of overall occupancy levels.

Beach access and waterfront living

Forest City has its own beach areas along the island perimeter, accessible to residents on foot from the residential zones. The waterfront promenade runs along sections of the island edge, providing a walking and cycling environment with open sea views. Recreational sea fishing is available from the waterfront and offshore areas. These are not features that exist in any gated township on the Johor mainland, however well-appointed.

Duty-free island living

Pulau 1 — the first and most developed of the four Forest City islands — operates under designated area status equivalent to Langkawi, Labuan, and Pangkor for duty-free purposes. This means certain goods including alcohol, tobacco, and selected consumer items are available at duty-free prices on the island. For residents, the practical effect is a lower cost of living on certain categories of daily expenditure — a detail that does not appear in property listings but accumulates meaningfully over years of residency.

The lifestyle case, without the investment argument

The financial case for Forest City depends on your outlook on the SFZ activation timeline, the secondary market recovery, and the pace of business establishment. Those are real variables with real uncertainty.

The lifestyle case does not have the same uncertainty. The island exists. The mangrove corridor exists. The two ranked golf courses exist and are running tournaments. The five-star hotel pools are open. The beach access is there. The duty-free designation is gazetted. None of this is speculative.

For buyers who choose Forest City primarily for the environment and lifestyle they want to live in — and see the SFZ policy framework as an additional tailwind rather than the primary rationale — that calculus is already favourable. The island is here. The question is whether it is the environment you want to come home to.
